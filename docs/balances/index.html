Balances

Equations for mass and energy balances.

Balancing equations are a way of bookkeeping for the main quantities involved in a process: mass, energy, and momentum. It comprises everything through which a quantity enters the system or leaves it, is produced or absorbed, and which resistances there are to the transport of the quantity. (Also see heat and mass transport.)

General balance of \(x\) :

$$\frac{dx}{dt}=\Phi_{x,in} - \Phi_{x,out} + \text{production}(x)$$

Where:

  • \(\frac{dx}{dt}\) is the rate of change of \(x\) in the system as a function of time. In a steady state, \(\frac{dx}{dt}=0\) .
  • \(\Phi_{x,in}\) is the rate of \(x\) entering the system.
  • \(\Phi_{x,out}\) is the rate of \(x\) leaving the system.
  • \(\text{production}(x)\) includes production and consumption or loss of \(x\) , and thus can be positive or negative.

Mass balance

Total mass balance: $$\frac{dm}{dt}=\Phi_{m,in} - \Phi_{m,out} + \text{production}(m)$$

With \(\Phi_m\) having the standard unit kg/s.

Since mass is volume times concentration ( \(m=Vc\) ) and volume times density ( \(m=V\rho\) ), the mass flow rate \(\Phi_m\) can be rewritten as \(\Phi_{V}c\) or \(\Phi_{V}\rho\) (with \(\Phi_{V}\) having the standard unit m^3/s, and \(c\) and \(\rho\) having the standard unit kg/m^3). Further, if the volume does not change, this becomes an equation of concentration:

$$V\frac{dc}{dt}=\Phi_{V,in}c_\text{in} - \Phi_{V,out}c_\text{out} + \text{production}(m)$$

For more on volume flow in fluids, see fluid flow.

Energy balance

Total energy balance:

$$\frac{de}{dt}=\Phi_{m,in}ec_\text{in} - \Phi_{m,out}ec_\text{out} + \Phi_q + \Phi_W$$

Where:

  • \(\Phi_W\) is the work done on the system.
  • \(\Phi_q\) is the heat flow into the system.
  • \(\Phi_{m,in\) is the mass inflow and \(\Phi_{m,out}\) is the mass outflow.
  • \(ec\) is the energy concentration:
$$ec=u+gh+\frac{1}{2}v^2+\frac{p}{\rho}$$

With:

  • \(u\) the internal energy
  • \(gh\) the potential energy (per mass unit)
  • \(\frac{1}{2}v^2\) the kinetic energy (per mass unit)
  • \(\frac{p}{\rho}\) the pressure energy of an incompressible fluid (where \(p\) the pressure and \(\rho\) the density)

The total energy balance can be split into mechanical and thermal energy.

Mechanical energy

$$\frac{de}{dt}=\Phi_{m,in}(gh+\frac{1}{2}v^2+\frac{p}{\rho})_\text{in} - \Phi_{m,out}(gh+\frac{1}{2}v^2+\frac{p}{\rho})_\text{out} + \Phi_W$$ (- energy lost from friction)

Neglecting friction, in a steady-state system with equal in- and outflow, where no work is performed, this can be simplified to Bernoulli’s equation: $$(gh+\frac{1}{2}v^2+\frac{p}{\rho})_\text{out}=(gh+\frac{1}{2}v^2+\frac{p}{\rho})_\text{in}$$

Thermal energy

$$\frac{de}{dt}=\Phi_{m,in} c_{p,\text{in}} T_\text{in} - \Phi_{m,out} c_{p,\text{out}} T_\text{out} + \Phi_q$$ (+ energy gained from friction)

For a primer on the different forms of energy, cf. the entry on energy.
